The basics

What is mental health?

Mental health includes our emotional, psychological, and social well-being. It affects how we think, feel, and act — and it's just as important as physical health.

Emotional well-being

The ability to understand and manage your feelings, cope with life's challenges, and bounce back from setbacks. Emotional health is about balance, not constant happiness.

Social well-being

Building and maintaining meaningful relationships, feeling connected to your community, and contributing positively to the world around you.

Psychological well-being

Having a sense of purpose, the ability to think clearly, learn new things, and make informed decisions. It's about growth, not perfection.

It's a spectrum

Mental health exists on a continuum. Everyone has mental health, and it can change day to day. Struggling doesn't mean you're "mentally ill" — it means you're human.

Self-perception

How you see and value yourself, including self-esteem and confidence. A healthy self-view supports growth and resilience.

Balance and resilience

The ability to maintain stability and adapt in the face of stress, adversity, or trauma. Resilience is a key component of mental health.

Holistic well-being

Mental health is interconnected with physical health, lifestyle, and environment. Taking care of your body, nurturing relationships, and creating a supportive environment all contribute to mental well-being.

Societal impact

Mental health isn't just an individual issue — it's a societal one. Stigma, discrimination, and lack of access to care affect communities and perpetuate cycles of suffering. Promoting mental health benefits everyone.

Dynamic and evolving

Our understanding of mental health is constantly evolving. What we know today is just the beginning — ongoing research continues to shed light on the complexities of the mind and how to support it.

Why it matters

Good mental health is the foundation for a fulfilling life. It allows us to build strong relationships, perform at our best, and navigate life's ups and downs with grace. Prioritizing mental health isn't a luxury — it's essential for everyone.

By understanding mental health, we can break down stigma, support each other, and create a world where everyone has the opportunity to thrive.

It's more than just "not being sick"

Mental health isn't just the absence of illness — it's about flourishing. It's about finding joy, meaning, and connection in life, even in the face of challenges. You can have a mental illness and still have good mental health in other areas of your life. It's about holistic well-being, not just symptom management.

Everyone's journey is unique

Mental health is deeply personal. What works for one person may not work for another, and that's okay. The important thing is to find what supports your well-being and to seek help when you need it. There's no "right" way to be mentally healthy — it's about finding your own path to wellness.

Common mental health conditions

Understanding these conditions reduces stigma and helps people seek help earlier.

Anxiety Disorders

Excessive worry, fear, or apprehension that interferes with daily activities. The most common mental health condition worldwide.

Worry Panic Avoidance Physical tension

Depression

More than sadness — it's a persistent low mood, loss of interest, and fatigue that affects all areas of life. Highly treatable.

Low mood Fatigue Sleep changes Hopelessness

PTSD

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder develops after traumatic events. It involves flashbacks, hypervigilance, and emotional numbness.

Flashbacks Nightmares Hypervigilance Avoidance

Bipolar Disorder

Characterized by extreme mood swings — from periods of intense highs (mania) to deep lows (depression). Manageable with treatment.

Mood swings Mania Depression Energy changes

OCD

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder involves unwanted, intrusive thoughts and repetitive behaviors performed to reduce anxiety.

Obsessions Compulsions Rituals

Eating Disorders

Complex mental health conditions involving troubled relationships with food, body image, and weight. Recovery is possible with support.

Body image Restriction Binging

Schizophrenia

A severe mental disorder affecting how a person thinks, feels, and behaves. It can involve hallucinations, delusions, and disorganized thinking.

Hallucinations Delusions Disorganized thinking

ADHD

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder is a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that interferes with functioning or development.

Inattention Hyperactivity Impulsivity

Personality Disorders

A group of mental health conditions characterized by enduring patterns of behavior, cognition, and inner experience that deviate from cultural expectations and cause distress or impairment.

Unstable relationships Impulsivity Emotional instability

Substance Use Disorders

A condition in which the use of one or more substances leads to a clinically significant impairment or distress, including health problems, disability, and failure to meet major responsibilities at work, school, or home.

Craving Loss of control Physical dependence

Autism Spectrum Disorder

A neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by challenges with social communication and interaction, and restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities.

Social communication difficulties Restricted interests Repetitive behaviors

Neurocognitive Disorders

A group of disorders characterized by a decline in cognitive function, including memory, attention, language, and problem-solving abilities, that interferes with daily functioning.

Memory loss Cognitive decline Impaired functioning
Know the signs

Warning signs to watch for

These signs don't always mean someone has a mental illness — but they're signals worth paying attention to, in yourself or others.

01
Withdrawal from others

Pulling away from friends, family, and activities once enjoyed.

02
Extreme mood changes

Unexplained highs and lows, or emotional outbursts out of proportion.

03
Difficulty with daily tasks

Struggling to carry out routine tasks like eating, sleeping, or work.

04
Feeling hopeless

Persistent feelings that things won't get better or that life isn't worth living.

05
Confused thinking

Trouble concentrating, remembering things, or making decisions.

06
Increased substance use

Using alcohol or drugs to cope with feelings or numb emotions.

07
Unexplained physical symptoms

Headaches, stomach problems, or other physical complaints without clear cause.

08
Thoughts of self-harm

Any thoughts of hurting yourself or others — always take these seriously.

09
Changes in sleep patterns

Sleeping too much or too little, or having trouble falling or staying asleep.

10
Loss of interest

No longer finding joy in activities or hobbies that used to be enjoyable.

11
Changes in appetite

Eating much more or much less than usual, or having significant weight changes.

12
Physical agitation

Restlessness, pacing, or an inability to sit still.

Busting stigma

Myths vs. facts

Stigma is one of the biggest barriers to people getting help. Let's set the record straight.

Myth

"Mental illness is a sign of weakness or personal failure."

Fact

Mental illness is caused by complex interactions of genetics, biology, environment, and life experiences — not weakness. It can affect anyone.

Myth

"People with mental illness are dangerous and unpredictable."

Fact

The vast majority of people with mental illness are not violent. In fact, they are more likely to be victims of violence than perpetrators.

Myth

"You can just 'snap out of it' if you try hard enough."

Fact

Mental illnesses are real medical conditions. Telling someone to "cheer up" is like telling someone with diabetes to just "produce more insulin."

Myth

"Therapy is only for people with serious problems."

Fact

Therapy benefits anyone who wants to better understand themselves, improve relationships, or develop coping skills — you don't need a diagnosis.

Myth

"Medication will change your personality or make you a zombie."

Fact

When properly prescribed and monitored, psychiatric medication helps restore balance — helping you feel more like yourself, not less.

Myth

"Mental illness is a lifelong sentence."

Fact

Many people recover completely from mental illness, and most can manage symptoms effectively with the right treatment and support.

Myth

"Only weak people need help."

Fact

Seeking help is a sign of strength and self-awareness. It takes courage to acknowledge when you need support and to take steps toward healing.
