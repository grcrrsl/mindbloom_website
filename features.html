Self-care toolkit

Evidence-based coping strategies

These strategies are backed by research. None replace professional help, but they can make a real difference in daily mental wellness.

Mindfulness

Paying attention to the present moment without judgment. Just 10 minutes daily can reduce stress and anxiety significantly.

Physical movement

Exercise releases endorphins, reduces cortisol, and improves sleep. Even a 20-minute walk has measurable mood benefits.

Journaling

Writing about thoughts and feelings helps process emotions, gain perspective, and track patterns in your mental health.

Sleep hygiene

Sleep and mental health are deeply linked. Consistent sleep schedules and wind-down routines can transform how you feel.

Social connection

Humans are wired for connection. Spending time with supportive people — even briefly — is a powerful buffer against mental illness.

Breathing exercises

Deep breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system. Try 4-7-8 breathing: inhale 4s, hold 7s, exhale 8s.

Creative expression

Engaging in creative activities like art, music, or writing can be a powerful way to process emotions and express yourself.

Nature exposure

Spending time in nature has been shown to reduce stress, improve mood, and enhance overall well-being.

Problem-solving

Breaking down large problems into smaller, manageable steps can reduce feelings of overwhelm and increase a sense of control.

Professional support

Seeking help from a mental health professional is a sign of strength, not weakness. Therapy and counseling can provide valuable tools and insights for managing mental health challenges.

Medication

When properly prescribed and monitored, psychiatric medication can be an effective treatment for many mental health conditions, helping to restore balance and improve quality of life.

Social support

Having a strong support network can make a significant difference in managing mental health challenges. Don't hesitate to reach out to friends, family, or support groups.

Self-Check

How are you feeling?

These short check-ins are not diagnostic tools — but they can help you recognise patterns and decide whether to seek support. Always consult a professional for a proper assessment.

Daily Mood Check-In

A quick 5-question pulse check on how you're doing today.

Over the last 2 weeks, how often have you felt little interest or pleasure in doing things?

How often have you felt down, depressed, or hopeless?

How often have you had trouble falling or staying asleep?

How often have you felt tired or had little energy?

How often have you felt nervous, anxious, or on edge?

This is a self-check only — not a clinical diagnosis. If you're concerned, please speak with a mental health professional.

A psychiatrist is a medical doctor who can diagnose mental health conditions and prescribe medication. A psychologist holds a doctoral degree in psychology and provides assessment and therapy but typically cannot prescribe medication (with some exceptions). A therapist or counsellor is a broader term covering licensed professionals (LCSWs, LMFTs, LPCs) who provide talk therapy. Many people work with both a psychiatrist for medication management and a therapist for ongoing support.

Consider therapy if your distress is persistent (lasting weeks), interfering with daily life, work, or relationships, or if you feel your friends are burdened. You don't need to be in crisis to benefit from therapy — many people use it for personal growth and self-understanding.

When properly prescribed and monitored, psychiatric medication is safe and effective. The goal of medication is not to change who you are — it's to reduce symptoms so you can function and feel more like yourself. All medications have potential side effects, which is why regular check-ins with your prescribing doctor are essential. Many people find that medication, combined with therapy, gives the best outcomes.

Types of Mental Health Professionals

Understanding who does what helps you find the right person for your specific needs — so you can get effective help faster.

Psychiatrist

MD / DO

Medical doctor specialising in mental health. Can diagnose conditions, prescribe and manage psychiatric medications, and provide therapy.

Good for: Medication management, complex diagnoses, severe conditions

Psychologist

PhD / PsyD / EdD

Doctoral-level clinician providing assessment, diagnosis, and evidence-based psychotherapy. Specialises in psychological testing and evaluation.

Good for: Assessment, CBT, trauma therapy, diagnostic clarity

Licensed Therapist

LCSW / LMFT / LPC

Master's-level clinician providing individual, couples, family, or group therapy. Wide range of specialisations available.

Good for: Talk therapy, relationship issues, life transitions, everyday mental health

Counsellor

MA / MS / MEd

Provides guidance and support for specific challenges such as grief, addiction, career, or school-related difficulties.

Good for: Grief, substance use, school issues, career stress

Social Worker

MSW / LCSW

Connects individuals and families with community resources, services, and support systems in addition to providing therapy.

Good for: Navigating systems, family support, crisis intervention

Peer Support Specialist

Certified Peer Support

A trained professional with lived experience of mental health challenges who provides support, connection, and hope to others on their journey.

Good for: Lived-experience support, reducing isolation, recovery communities

Common Therapy Approaches

CBT
Cognitive Behavioural Therapy

Identifies and reshapes unhelpful thought patterns and behaviours. The most evidence-based therapy for anxiety and depression.

EMDR
Eye Movement Desensitisation & Reprocessing

Uses bilateral stimulation to help process and integrate traumatic memories. Highly effective for PTSD.

DBT
Dialectical Behaviour Therapy

Teaches emotional regulation, distress tolerance, mindfulness, and interpersonal effectiveness. Originally developed for BPD.

ACT
Acceptance & Commitment Therapy

Focuses on accepting difficult thoughts and feelings rather than fighting them, while committing to value-driven action.

IFS
Internal Family Systems

Explores the different "parts" of yourself — including wounded inner children and protective parts — to foster self-leadership.

MBT
Mindfulness-Based Therapy

Integrates mindfulness meditation with cognitive therapy to break cycles of rumination and prevent depression relapse.
